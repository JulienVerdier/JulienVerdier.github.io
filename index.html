Who I am

Mostly an immunologist, fascinated by the complexity of the immune system and profoundly enjoying coding, data analysis and data visualisation. More specifically, I obtained my PhD in immunology at Paris 6 university. My PhD was about the role of the Th17 response in inflammatory bowel diseases. After my PhD, I did a first post-doc in Germany in the European consortium SysmedIBD. After that I came back to France, where I used mass cytometry (see the french version of the Wikipedia article I wrote about this technique) to characterize circulating immune cells in Myasthenia gravis, a rare autoimmune disease.

I am a wet-lab scientist, extensively trained to make cells talk, but I can also code scripts for analyses that cannot be done otherwise.

Complexity in biology : the immunology case

When people talk about complexity in biology, they often refer to our brain. Somehow, the brain is truely complex as it is endowed with at least one obvious emerging property : cognition. And there are many neurons: 1011, which is litteraly an astronomical number as this is the number of galaxies in the universe (and the number of stars in the milky way, our galaxy). However, there are 10 times more immune cells as there are neurons. One might argue that neurons are largely interconnected, but so is the case for immune cells, these cells communicate with each other throughout the whole body.

Complexity of immunology is in sharp contrast with claims of precision medicine, which is a wonderful goal but we are still far from it.
